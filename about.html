Who I Am

I'm Alan Lamb, an applied economist and data scientist completing my M.S. in Applied Economics at the University of Maryland, with a focus on forecasting, causal inference, and data-driven decision support. I also hold an MPA from the University of Montana.

Before economics, I spent 14 years managing bars and restaurants — which taught me more about demand forecasting, operational decisions under uncertainty, and managing people than most classrooms do. I know what it looks like when a model has to be right, and when the cost of being wrong is immediate.

What I Work On

I build forecasting pipelines in Python and causal inference models in R. On the forecasting side, I use Monte Carlo simulation, time-series decomposition, and ensemble methods to quantify uncertainty across financial markets, commodities, and economic indicators. On the causal side, I use difference-in-differences, event studies, and synthetic control designs to evaluate policies and natural experiments.

My data work lives in SQL, Python, and R. I publish everything to GitHub so the methodology is fully transparent and reproducible — the code is part of the deliverable, not an afterthought.

How I Work

My approach is grounded in probabilistic thinking, calibrated uncertainty, and a preference for methods that are transparent and replicable. I document assumptions explicitly so results can be scrutinized, updated, and handed off — not just presented once and forgotten.

I participate in structured forecasting through Good Judgment Open and Metaculus, where I track my calibration against resolved questions. That practice keeps me honest about the difference between confidence and accuracy — and builds a public track record of both.

Why I Built This Site

Lambcast is my public portfolio and working lab. If you're an employer or collaborator, this site is designed to show you how I think, how I build, and how I handle uncertainty. The forecasts are live and revisable. The methodology is documented. The code is public. That's intentional — I want the work to be evaluated, not just described.
